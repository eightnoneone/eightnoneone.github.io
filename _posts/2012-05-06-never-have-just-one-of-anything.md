We went up to NH to visit my mom and retrieve my dad's car from it's winter storage. I figured the tires had probably lost some air sitting all winter. No problem – Pete has compressed air piped out to the garage and a 50 foot hose reel at the ready. Of course.

Then I remembered the compressor wasn't running due to a switch problem. With no other projects at hand, now was as good a time as any. I was going to move the compressor to my place eventually plus it would be handy to have working during the move and cleanup of his shop.

Now that's a switch.

No surprise for those that knew Pete, this was not a simple switch. The primary switch is and Allen-Bradley 609 Industrial manual motor control switch. Out of a Kingsbury scrap bin and thus way oversized. Two very satisfying buttons, a green one labeled START and a red one says STOP. Complete with 1950's styling. It has a pressure switch wired in from the tank and a relay wired to the master light switch for the shop. Turn out the lights and you kill the compressor too. Naturally.

![Allen Bradley 609](/assets/posts/allen-bradley-609.jpg)

This means the problem could be in a number of different places. The compressor is also a relic. It's been there since I can remember. I'm often asked if I can feel the spirit of my father. Most know spirits aren't my thing. I can go to the file cabinet, open a folder and find my dad right there. A note written in 1996 for someone looking to get a grip on this antique. Every spec, requirement, and even the back-story.

"Trade deal w/Rob Doody sometime in 1973-4. Was owned by Bob Doody Sr. and used by him in shops in Keene by river @ Rt 101 and in center of Marlboro next to fire station."

In diagnosing the problem I discovered the compressor would run if I held the start button down. So it's not the pressure switch or the lighting relay. That removes much of the complication. I also found that the plastic casing around the switch mechanics are all cracked broken away. Looks like a replacement switch is going to be the easiest fix.

I started looking for new or refurbished ones online. Allen-Bradley still make the switch but even smaller single phase, 110v ones are $400 or more new. Some refirbs were $180. Ug.

The then thought came to me. Pete never had one of anything. There had to be some spare or similar switches around somewhere. I didn't have to go far before finding two identical units in the shop. Cleaned up one, took the guts out and it fit right into the housing on the wall. Wired back up again and in no time we have 120 PSI.

![Ingersoll Rand Type 30](/assets/posts/compressor-flywheel.jpg)

Eighty plus gallon's of 120 PSI, of course.
