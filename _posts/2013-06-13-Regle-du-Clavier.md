Father's Day got me thinking about my dad, as it should. I recently called a photographer that had come to my dad's shop to photograph him while he worked. The pictures he took are some I cherish most. It's the focused, silent, master at his tools. The photographer didn't know my dad before the shoot. He was an amateur photographer looking to do an essay of a craftsman. He's asked a mutual friend if he knew anyone that might be willing to host him for an afternoon. Pete and his cave were an easy choice.

![Regle du Clavier](/assets/posts/regle-du-clavier.jpg){: width="300" height="300" .right }
Remembering this story today got me thinking about the times Pete collaborated with or help artists. I don't think he would have appeared to most as a patron of the arts. He enjoyed classical music, piano concertos almost exclusively. I don't know if I ever knew him to want to go to an art museum. As you might expect, any analysis of art usually came down to the mechanics or the medium or the skills. This was how he could intersect with domains outside his own.

That photographer told me he got far more than he expected that day. He witnessed a person so clearly a master as what he was doing. For me it is captured in a watercolor painting done by an artist Pete helped one day. Marie was a puppeteer and painter that came to my dad for help with some puppet mechanics, as I remember. He was enthralledRegle-du-Clavier see someone so good at her craft and more than happy to help.

As a thank-you and for his birthday, she painted this watercolor. It's of the centerpiece machine in his shop. A vintage Bridgeport Series 1 milling machine. She wrote below "Regle du Clavier." I've never know the colloquial translation (please tell me if you do).

I've always understood it to mean "master of instruments". That, he was.

Happy Father's Day, Dad.
